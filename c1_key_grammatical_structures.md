## Key Grammatical Structures for C1 Level

These are the main advanced features expected at C1 (from British Council, Cambridge English, Tracktest, and other CEFR-aligned sources). Master them with natural use, not just rote learning.

### 1. Advanced Conditionals (including Mixed and Inverted)

- All types (zero, first, second, third) + mixed conditionals
  - e.g., past actions affecting present: *"If I had studied harder, I would be more confident now."*
- Inverted forms for emphasis/formality: *"Had I known..., I would have..."* (instead of "If I had known...")
- Alternatives to "if": provided that, unless, supposing, on condition that, as long as

### 2. Participle Clauses (for conciseness and sophistication)

- **Present participle**: *"Having finished the lecture, I took notes."* (instead of "After I finished...")
- **Past participle**: *"Built in the 19th century, the bridge still stands."*
- **Perfect participle**: *"Having been warned about the risks, she proceeded carefully."*

These make your Speaking/Writing more advanced and fluid.

### 3. Passive Voice (Advanced Forms and in All Tenses)

- **Complex passives**: *"It is believed that the policy will be implemented soon."* / *"The report is said to have been falsified."*
- **Causative passive**: *"I had my computer repaired."*
- Use for formality/academic tone in Writing (Integrated task) or Speaking (Task 4)

### 4. Inversion (Especially with Negative Adverbials)

- **For emphasis**: *"Never have I seen such a beautiful city."* / *"Not only did she pass, but she also got the highest score."*
- With adverbs like: seldom, rarely, hardly, no sooner... than..., only then...
- Great for Speaking emphasis or Writing variety

### 5. Cleft Sentences (for Emphasis)

- **It-cleft**: *"It was the professor who inspired me."* (not "The professor inspired me.")
- **What-cleft**: *"What surprised me was how quickly time passed."*
- **All-cleft**: *"All I need is more practice."*

These highlight key ideasâ€”perfect for TOEFL Speaking responses.

### 6. Modals in the Past / Advanced Modals (Speculation, Deduction, Regret)

- Must have / might have / could have / should have + past participle
  - e.g., *"He must have forgotten the meeting."*
- Needn't have / didn't need to (regret vs. no obligation)

### 7. Reported Speech / Reporting Verbs (Advanced)

- With nuanced verbs: suggest, insist, recommend, deny, admit + gerund/infinitive/clause
- Backshifting + no backshifting in truths/universal facts

### 8. Ellipsis and Substitution (Avoiding Repetition)

- Omit repeated words: *"Some people prefer online classes, and others face-to-face ones."* â†’ *"Some people prefer online classes, others face-to-face."*
- So / neither / do / do so / one / do the same

### 9. Hedging and Boosting Language (for Academic/Nuanced Tone)

- **Hedging**: It seems/appears that..., arguably, potentially, to some extent
- **Boosting**: undoubtedly, clearly, significantly
- Essential for TOEFL Writing (expressing balanced opinions) and Speaking (Task 3/4)

### 11. Subjunctive Mood (Formal Recommendations and Demands)

- Used in formal/academic contexts for recommendations, demands, suggestions
- **Structure**: It is essential/vital/important that + subject + **base verb** (no "s", no "should")
  - e.g., *"The committee recommended that he **submit** his proposal immediately."* (not "submits")
  - *"It is crucial that every participant **be** present."* (not "is")
- Common triggers: suggest, recommend, insist, demand, propose, require, request, it is essential/vital/imperative that...
- Perfect for TOEFL Writing Task 2 when discussing policies or recommendations

### 12. Nominalization (Academic Writing Power Tool)

- Converting verbs/adjectives into nouns for formal, concise academic tone
- **Examples**:
  - *"The government **decided** to reform"* â†’ *"The government's **decision** to reform"*
  - *"Children are naturally **curious**"* â†’ *"Children's natural **curiosity**"*
  - *"The economy **grew** rapidly"* â†’ *"Rapid economic **growth**"*
- Makes writing denser and more sophisticatedâ€”essential for TOEFL Integrated Writing
- Common patterns: -tion (creation), -ment (development), -ness (awareness), -ity (complexity)

### 13. Sentential Relative Clauses (Commenting on Whole Ideas)

- Using "which" to refer to an entire previous clause, not just a noun
- **Examples**:
  - *"The university increased tuition fees, **which angered many students**."*
  - *"She finished her thesis early, **which was unexpected**."*
- More sophisticated than simple coordination (*"...and this angered..."*)
- Adds nuance and commentaryâ€”great for Speaking Task 4 summaries

### 14. Reduced Relative Clauses (Advanced Concision)

- Omitting relative pronoun + auxiliary for streamlined sentences
- **Present participle reduction**:
  - *"Students **who are studying** abroad"* â†’ *"Students **studying** abroad"*
- **Past participle reduction**:
  - *"Issues **which are related** to climate"* â†’ *"Issues **related** to climate"*
  - *"The data **that was collected**"* â†’ *"The data **collected**"*
- Makes speech and writing more fluent and less repetitive

### 15. Fronting for Emphasis (Beyond Inversion)

- Moving elements to sentence-initial position for dramatic effect or focus
- **Object fronting**: *"This theory, I cannot accept."* (vs. "I cannot accept this theory.")
- **Predicate fronting**: *"So rare was this opportunity that many applied."*
- **Complement fronting**: *"A genius he certainly was not."*
- More advanced than standard inversionâ€”use sparingly for maximum impact in Speaking/Writing

### 16. Future Perfect & Future Continuous (Precise Time Relationships)

- **Future Perfect**: Actions completed before a future point
  - *"By next month, I **will have completed** all my coursework."*
  - *"In 2030, scientists **will have discovered** new treatments."*
- **Future Continuous**: Actions in progress at a future time
  - *"This time next week, I **will be taking** my TOEFL exam."*
  - *"Researchers **will be analyzing** the results throughout next year."*
- Show sophisticated grasp of time relationshipsâ€”useful for TOEFL Speaking Task 1 (future plans)

## How to Apply These in TOEFL for C1

### Speaking (Target 25+ for C1)

Use 2â€“3 advanced structures per response. For example, in Independent Task:
- Start with a cleft: *"What I value most is..."*
- Add a mixed conditional: *"If technology hadn't advanced so rapidly, we wouldn't have..."*
- End with inversion for emphasis: *"Only then did I realize..."*

Record and self-check for natural delivery.

### Writing (Keep/Maintain 28+)

- **Integrated Task**: Use participle clauses for summarizing (*"Having listened to the lecture, I believe..."*)
- **Independent Task**: Employ passives, clefts, and hedging (*"It is widely argued that..."*)

### Listening/Reading

Exposure to these in lectures/passages helps recognition.
